# 2. Introduction

## 2.1 Background

Software has become an essential part of almost every industry, including healthcare, finance, manufacturing, education, transportation, and government services. As software systems continue to grow in complexity, organizations face increasing pressure to deliver high-quality products more quickly while adapting to constantly changing customer needs.

Traditional software development methodologies, such as the Waterfall Model, follow a sequential process in which requirements are gathered, the system is designed, implemented, tested, and finally deployed. While this approach works well for projects with stable and well-defined requirements, it often struggles when customer expectations change during development.

Modern software development requires greater flexibility, faster feedback, and continuous collaboration between stakeholders and development teams. These needs led to the emergence of Agile software development methodologies.

---

## 2.2 Agile Software Development

Agile is a project management and software development philosophy that emphasizes flexibility, collaboration, customer satisfaction, and continuous improvement. Rather than attempting to deliver an entire product at the end of a project, Agile encourages teams to deliver working software in small, incremental releases.

The Agile Manifesto, published in 2001 by seventeen software practitioners, defines four core values that continue to guide Agile development today:

- Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
- Working software over comprehensive documentation
- Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
- Responding to change over following a plan

These principles encourage teams to remain adaptable while maintaining close communication with customers throughout the development lifecycle.

---

## 2.3 What is Scrum?

Scrum is one of the most popular Agile frameworks used to manage complex projects. Originally developed for software engineering, Scrum is now applied across a wide range of industries, including manufacturing, healthcare, education, finance, and marketing.

Unlike traditional project management methodologies, Scrum divides work into short, fixed-length iterations known as **Sprints**, typically lasting between one and four weeks. At the end of each Sprint, the team delivers a usable product increment that provides measurable value to stakeholders.

Scrum focuses on:

- Collaboration among team members
- Continuous customer feedback
- Incremental product delivery
- Adaptability to changing requirements
- Continuous improvement through inspection and reflection

The framework is intentionally lightweight, providing only the essential structure needed for teams to organize their work while allowing flexibility in implementation.

---

## 2.4 Why Scrum?

Organizations adopt Scrum because it helps them respond more effectively to changing business environments.

Some of the primary reasons for adopting Scrum include:

- Faster delivery of valuable software
- Improved customer satisfaction
- Better communication within development teams
- Increased transparency throughout the project
- Early identification of project risks
- Continuous quality improvement
- Greater flexibility when requirements change
- Higher employee engagement through self-organizing teams

Instead of waiting months or years before customers see a finished product, Scrum enables organizations to gather feedback after every Sprint and continuously refine the product.

---

## 2.5 Key Characteristics of Scrum

Scrum is built around several defining characteristics that distinguish it from traditional development methodologies.

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| Iterative Development | Work is completed through short Sprints. |
| Incremental Delivery | Each Sprint produces a usable product increment. |
| Customer Collaboration | Stakeholders provide regular feedback. |
| Transparency | Work progress is visible to everyone. |
| Inspection | Teams regularly review progress and product quality. |
| Adaptation | Teams continuously improve based on feedback. |
| Cross-functional Teams | Members possess all necessary skills to deliver value. |
| Self-Organization | Teams determine how best to accomplish their work. |

---

## 2.6 Scrum Beyond Software Development

Although Scrum originated in software engineering, its principles are applicable to many other fields.

Today Scrum is successfully used in:

- Healthcare
- Banking and Finance
- Government organizations
- Manufacturing
- Product Development
- Marketing
- Education
- Research
- Construction
- Startups

The framework's adaptability makes it suitable for any environment where work involves uncertainty, collaboration, and continuous improvement.

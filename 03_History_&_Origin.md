# 3. History and Origin of Scrum

## 3.1 Introduction

Scrum is one of the most influential Agile frameworks used in modern project management and software development. Although it is widely associated with software engineering today, its origins extend beyond the software industry. Scrum evolved from ideas in manufacturing, product development, and organizational management, where collaboration, adaptability, and continuous improvement were recognized as essential for managing complex work.

Understanding the history of Scrum provides valuable insight into why the framework was created and how its principles continue to guide organizations around the world.

---

## 3.2 The Need for a New Development Approach

During the 1970s and 1980s, software projects were primarily managed using traditional sequential methodologies such as the Waterfall Model. These approaches required extensive planning before development began and assumed that customer requirements would remain stable throughout the project.

However, many organizations encountered recurring problems:

- Changing customer requirements during development
- Long delivery cycles
- High project failure rates
- Poor communication between customers and developers
- Late discovery of defects
- Expensive changes during later project phases

As software systems became larger and more complex, these limitations highlighted the need for a more flexible and adaptive development process.

---

## 3.3 Origins of the Scrum Concept

The term **"Scrum"** was inspired by the sport of rugby, where players work closely together as a unified team to move the ball toward a common goal.

In 1986, **Hirotaka Takeuchi** and **Ikujiro Nonaka** introduced the concept in their influential Harvard Business Review article:

> **"The New New Product Development Game"**

Rather than describing software development, the article examined successful product development practices in companies such as:

- Honda
- Canon
- Fuji-Xerox
- NEC
- Epson

The authors observed that the highest-performing teams shared several common characteristics:

- Cross-functional collaboration
- Shared responsibility
- Continuous learning
- Fast decision making
- Incremental development
- Strong customer focus

They compared these highly collaborative teams to a rugby scrum, where players move together toward a common objective.

This publication laid the conceptual foundation for what would later become the Scrum framework.

---

## 3.4 Development of Scrum

Building upon these ideas, **Ken Schwaber** and **Jeff Sutherland** developed Scrum as a practical framework for software development during the early 1990s.

Their objective was to create a lightweight framework capable of handling uncertainty while improving productivity and software quality.

The first public presentation of Scrum took place at the **OOPSLA Conference** in 1995, where Schwaber and Sutherland introduced Scrum as a new approach to software project management.

The framework emphasized:

- Small, self-organizing teams
- Short development cycles (Sprints)
- Continuous customer feedback
- Regular inspection and adaptation
- Incremental software delivery

These concepts represented a significant departure from traditional project management methodologies.

---

## 3.5 Scrum and the Agile Movement

Although Scrum was already gaining popularity during the 1990s, it became even more influential after the publication of the **Agile Manifesto** in 2001.

Seventeen software practitioners met in Snowbird, Utah, to discuss alternatives to heavyweight software development methodologies. Their collaboration resulted in the Agile Manifesto, which established four core values and twelve guiding principles for Agile software development.

Scrum naturally aligned with these values by emphasizing:

- Customer collaboration
- Working software
- Adaptability
- Continuous improvement
- Empowered development teams

Today, Scrum is considered one of the most widely adopted Agile frameworks.

---

## 3.6 The Scrum Guide

As Scrum adoption increased worldwide, there was a need for a standardized description of the framework.

To address this need, **Ken Schwaber** and **Jeff Sutherland** published the first official **Scrum Guide** in **2010**.

The Scrum Guide defines:

- Scrum values
- Scrum roles
- Scrum events
- Scrum artifacts
- Rules governing the framework

The guide is intentionally concise and serves as the official reference for Scrum practitioners.

Since its initial publication, the Scrum Guide has undergone several revisions to reflect evolving industry practices.

The most recent version was released in **2020**, emphasizing simplicity, product thinking, and value delivery.

---

## 3.7 Evolution of Scrum

Over the past three decades, Scrum has evolved beyond software development into a framework used across many industries.

Today, Scrum supports projects in:

- Healthcare
- Finance
- Manufacturing
- Marketing
- Government
- Education
- Research
- Construction
- Product Design
- Artificial Intelligence

Organizations use Scrum not only to develop software but also to manage innovation, improve collaboration, and accelerate product delivery.

## 3.9 Impact on Modern Project Management

Today, Scrum has become one of the world's most widely implemented project management frameworks.

Thousands of organizations—including Google, Microsoft, Spotify, Amazon, IBM, Atlassian, and many startups—use Scrum to improve collaboration, accelerate product delivery, and respond effectively to changing customer requirements.

Its success demonstrates that flexible planning, empowered teams, and continuous learning can significantly improve project outcomes in uncertain environments.
